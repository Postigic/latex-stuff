\begin{claim}
    You can tile an $8 \times 8$ chessboard with $32$ dominoes.
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}
    Partition the board into rows. Each row consists of 8 squares,
    which can be tiled with 4 horizontal dominoes placed end-to-end.
    Applying this to all 8 rows yields a valid tiling using 
    $8 \times 4 = 32$ dominoes, covering all $8 \times 8 = 64$ squares
    with no overlap.
\end{proof}

\begin{claim}
    You cannot tile an $8 \times 8$ chessboard with $31$ dominoes
    if the opposite corners are removed.
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}
    On a standard chessboard, there are 32 black and 32 white squares, 
    and the two opposite corners share the same colour. Without loss of
    generality, assume both are white. Removing them leaves 32 black
    and 30 white squares.

    \medskip Every domino covers exactly one black square and one white square,
    so any tiling by 31 dominoes covers exactly 31 black and 31 white squares. 
    This contradicts the board having 32 black and 30 white squares. 
    Therefore no such tiling exists.
\end{proof}
